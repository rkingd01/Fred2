Persecution

'Kulaks' was a derogatory term for the relatively affluent peasant class and 'de-kulakization' referred to their 'removal'.

'Removal' meant deportation including the confiscation of all possessions. Many were executed by Cheka (NKVD) and their offspring placed in large orphanages.

The Georgijev and Noole families were comparatively affluent living in a rural Finnic community of about 127,000 according to Lutheran records.

Finns and Estonians spoke their own language, worshipped in their own churches and were educated in their own schools.

Ingria was historically home to a large Finnic community and before the October Revolution 1917 there had been a freedom of movement in and out of Russia.


The nascent Bolshevik government was primarily concerned with recurring food shortages and counter-revolution. Lenin introduced 'cooperatives' where food could be bought at artificially low prices but land owners refused to drop their prices to such low levels leaving the cooperatives without sufficient stock. People continued to starve.

When Stalin became head of state he demonised this affluent peasant class, maligned them as 'kulaks' and on December 27, 1920 openly announced plans for their "liquidation".


State-wide propaganda successfully characterised this class as evil, enjoying prosperity whilst the rest of the population suffered extreme hardship. The damnation of this class was overwhelming and even sprouted its own art form.
